The idea of this application web is to visualize and analyze the effect and the influence of some factors from different categories (immunization, mortality, economic, social) on life expectancy in the world. In addition, we will compare the life expectancy between all countries

This chart represents a bubble chart. The gross domestic product (GDP) is on the x-axis and life expectancy on the y axis, whereas the bubble size is represented by the population of each country. Countries belonging to the same content will have the same color. From the chart, we can note that the western countries (Europe, North America) plus Australia and New Zeeland, Singapore, Japan, and North Korea have an average GDP over the years (from 2000 to 2015) between 8K to 90k. Asia has an average GDP below 10k. On the other hand, the GDP of golf countries such as Qatar, and Kuwait increased over the years to overcome the 10K . Africa has an average GDP below 7k, which makes it the lowest GDP across the other continents. Those results show the effect of the GDP on the life span of humanity as the more GDP the individual gets, the more his age increases and vice versa.

Social factor

The impact of Schooling on the lifespan of human

Life expectancy: Life Expectancy in age

Schooling: Number of years of Schooling(years)

This represents a correlation line chart. It describes the relationship between life expectancy (on the left y-axis) and schooling factor (on the right y-axis) over the years from 2000 to 2015. The chart shows that the number of years in school affects the life expectancy of humans. As the number of years in school increases, life expectancy increases as well . We notice that for example the schooling value in Africa is less than in Europe, which implies a less life span in Africa compared to Europe. Leaving school early pushes children to hard work at a young age or to resort to the world of crime, and all this leads to damaging their health and thus a negative impact on human life.

Immunization factors

The effect of Hepatitis B coverage

Hepatitis B: (HepB) immunization coverage among 1-year-olds (%)

The effect of Polio coverage

Polio: (Pol3) immunization coverage among 1-year-olds (%)

The effect of Diphtheria coverage

Diphtheria: Diphtheria tetanus toxoid and pertussis (DTP3) immunization coverage among 1-year-olds (%)

These charts represent correlation line charts of immunization factors. It describes the correlation between life expectancy (on the left y-axis) and immunization factors (on the right y-axis) over the years from 2000 to 2015. The immunization factors are Hepatitis B, Polio, and Diphtheria coverage among 1-year-olds . The charts show that when the percentage of immunization coverage of each factor is higher , life expectancy increases as well . We notice that the immunization coverage in developing countries is less than the developed countries. These results justify the outcome we saw in the choropleth map where the average life expectancy in Africa and Asia and some countries in south America was less compared to other countries such as the European ones.

percentage expenditure: Expenditure on health as a percentage of Gross Domestic Product per capita(%)

The effect of total expenditure coverage

These charts represent correlation line charts of economic factors. Mortality factors

The impact of adult mortality

Adult Mortality: Adult Mortality Rates of both sexes (probability of dying between 15 and 60 years per 1000 population)

The impact of infant deaths

Infant deaths: Number of Infant Deaths per 1000 population

These charts represent correlation line charts of mortality factors. It describes the correlation between life expectancy (on the left y-axis) and mortality factors (on the right y-axis) over the years from 2000 to 2015. The mortality factors are the probability of dying between 15 and 60 years per 1000 population and the number of Infant Deaths per 1000 population . The charts show that when both factors are lower , life expectancy increases. We notice that in developing countries, infant deaths and adult mortality are higher than the developed countries. This is reflected in the average life expectancy over the years as the choropleth map showed.
